The Dawn of

Creativity

Deep within the caves of Asia, early humans dipped their hands into pigments made from crushed minerals and plants. By firelight, they painted mammoths, bison, and hunting scenes on the rocky walls. This was the birth of art, a primal urge to capture and understand the world.

Neolithic Age

The Transition:
Caves to Civilizations

As the Ice Age gave way to a warmer world, humans began to step out of the caves and into the light, carrying with them the lessons of creation that they've learned in the shadows.

The art that began on cave walls would soon evolve into something even more extraordinary.

In the fertile valleys of Mesopotamia, the riverbanks of Egypt, and the highlands of the Andes, early civilizations began to flourish. They built temples, carved statues, and painted murals that told the stories of their gods, their kings, and their people.

However With Every

Civilization That Rise,
A Fall Awaits

It was rumored that the Macedonian army had crossed the Granicus river, allowing the mighty King, Alexander the Great to advance towards the capital of the acient Kingdom of Persia, Persepolis.

Persepolis was a city of wonders. Its towering columns seemed hold up the sky. Yet none of that mattered to Alexander, who burned the city to the ground. Hundreds of years of art and literature was lost to the ashes of the great fire.
